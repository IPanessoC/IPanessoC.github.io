Summary of AI Threats

Artificial Intelligence has evolved beyond text generation to clone voices and create hyper-realistic faces in seconds. Cybercriminals use these tools for scams, credential theft, and manipulation:

Summary of AI Threats
  • Voice Cloning (Vishing): Cloning a family member's voice to demand urgent money.
  • Next-Generation Phishing: Creating fake videos of authority figures or CEOs asking to verify data or click links.
  • Manipulation: Spreading disinformation campaigns to polarize opinions or alter financial markets.
How to Detect AI-Generated Content
  • Images: Look for defective anatomy (extra/missing fingers), nonsense text in the background, and impossible lighting or shadows.
  • Audio: Listen for a lack of vital pauses (no breathing sounds), robotic tones during emergencies, and metallic background noise.
  • Video: Watch for unnatural blinking, lip-sync desynchronization, and blurry edges around the face, hair, and ears.
Cybersecurity Habits and Verification
  • Cross-Verification: Search for news on Google to see if reliable media report it, and use reverse image search tools (like Google Lens or TinEye) for suspicious pictures.
  • Zero Trust Principle: If a message triggers a strong emotion (fear, urgency, indignation), pause immediately, as criminals want victims to act without thinking.
  • Family Safe Word: Agree on a secret code word with family members to prevent AI phone scams.
  • Digital Footprint: Protect your identity by making videos and audio less public on social media and reviewing account privacy settings.
Conclusions

In the AI era, doubting is our best antivirus. The most effective defense is to pause, think, and verify before sharing, clicking, or transferring anything.

Opinion

Can you imagine receiving a call from your child desperately asking for money, only to find out later that it was a computer-generated voice clone? Or attending a business video call with your boss, transferring millions of dollars, and then realizing your boss was a hyper-realistic deepfake?
These kinds of scenarios are no longer science fiction. AI-assisted scams have become cybercriminals' favorite tools, forcing governments worldwide to react. The legal gray area is shrinking fast. Today, using AI to scam someone or steal their identity can cost years in prison and millions in fines.
Here is a look at how global justice is cornering these new criminals and what penalties they face.

Opinion

The New Legal Front: From Corporate Fines to Prison Time

For years, scammers took advantage of outdated technology laws. However, criminal codes have changed dramatically to classify the use of AI as an aggravating factor. This means that committing a "traditional" scam using deepfakes or voice cloning drastically multiplies the years behind bars.
This is how this crime is being punished around the world:

🇨🇴 Colombia: A Regional Leader with Law 2502

Colombia made history by passing Law 2502. This regulation introduces the legal concept of Deepfakes and updates the Criminal Code (Article 296) to turn identity theft through AI into an aggravating circumstance.

  • The consequences: Criminals who use cloned videos or voices to impersonate someone will not only face charges for identity theft or fraud, but their sentences and financial fines will automatically increase by up to one-third (33%). Furthermore, the country now legally protects a person's "external image" (their face, body, and voice) against unauthorized manipulation.
🇪🇺 The European Union: Massive Fines Under the "AI Act"

The European Union means business. With its Artificial Intelligence Act fully active, the focus is both on the scammers and the tech companies that build the software.

  • The consequences: Creating or sharing deepfakes without a clear label warning that the content is artificial carries administrative fines of up to 35 million euros or 7% of a company's global turnover. For criminals using these tools for financial fraud, European courts apply combined sentences for cybercrime and aggravated fraud, which can lead to 6 to 8 years in prison, depending on the amount of money stolen.
🇺🇸 The United States: Federal Prison and Identity Theft

In the US, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and the Department of Justice are cracking down on voice cloning through schemes like Vishing or BEC (Business Email Compromise) fraud.

  • consequences: Prosecutors are charging scammers with federal offenses like Wire Fraud and Aggravated Identity Theft. A cybercriminal who uses AI to trick a company or an elderly person can face a mandatory 2-year additional prison sentence just for the identity theft charge, on top of a base sentence for wire fraud that can reach up to 20 years in federal prison.
The Three Most Punished AI Scams
  1. CEO Fraud (Fake Video Calls): The criminal creates a digital video clone of an executive and orders employees to make transfers. Since this is a highly planned financial fraud, courts usually hand down maximum sentences without bail.
  2. Synthetic Sextortion: Creating fake explicit images of a victim to blackmail them. Courts (like those in Spain or the US) treat this under digital violence and non-consensual pornography laws, resulting in automatic 3 to 5-year prison terms, even if the material was never published online.
  3. Voice Cloning for Virtual Kidnappings: The criminal calls a family imitating the voice of a relative. Judges process this as aggravated extortion through psychological terror, which significantly increases minimum sentences.
Conclusion: The End of Digital Impunity

Artificial Intelligence moves fast, but justice is finally catching up. The message from courts around the world is clear: AI is no longer a gray zone for committing anonymous crimes. Using a voice algorithm or video editing software to steal or extort is considered highly premeditated, and prison systems worldwide already have cells ready for those who cross that digital line.
As users, our best defense is still prevention: set up double-verification methods and double-check any unusual requests for money, even if the voice on the other end sounds exactly like a loved one.
