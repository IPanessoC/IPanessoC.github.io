In this post, I want to share some of my knowledge as a cybersecurity analyst. I still have a lot to learn and practice,
but I feel a great affinity for sharing information about best practices for users and cybersecurity education.
So, despite not being in English, I would like to share this infographic in Spanish about the dangers in the AI era, along with some ideas on how to prevent them.

Summary of AI Threats

Artificial Intelligence has evolved beyond text generation to clone voices and create hyper-realistic faces in seconds. Cybercriminals use these tools for scams, credential theft, and manipulation:

Summary of AI Threats
  • Voice Cloning (Vishing): Cloning a family member's voice to demand urgent money.
  • Next-Generation Phishing: Creating fake videos of authority figures or CEOs asking to verify data or click links.
  • Manipulation: Spreading disinformation campaigns to polarize opinions or alter financial markets.
How to Detect AI-Generated Content
  • Images: Look for defective anatomy (extra/missing fingers), nonsense text in the background, and impossible lighting or shadows.
  • Audio: Listen for a lack of vital pauses (no breathing sounds), robotic tones during emergencies, and metallic background noise.
  • Video: Watch for unnatural blinking, lip-sync desynchronization, and blurry edges around the face, hair, and ears.
Cybersecurity Habits and Verification
  • Cross-Verification: Search for news on Google to see if reliable media report it, and use reverse image search tools (like Google Lens or TinEye) for suspicious pictures.
  • Zero Trust Principle: If a message triggers a strong emotion (fear, urgency, indignation), pause immediately, as criminals want victims to act without thinking.
  • Family Safe Word: Agree on a secret code word with family members to prevent AI phone scams.
  • Digital Footprint: Protect your identity by making videos and audio less public on social media and reviewing account privacy settings.
Conclusions

In the AI era, doubting is our best antivirus. The most effective defense is to pause, think, and verify before sharing, clicking, or transferring anything.
